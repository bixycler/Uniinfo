Exponential

With identity \( a^{x+y} = a^x a^y \), exponential bridges between addition and multiplication, and with identity \( \d a^x = a^x\d x \), exponential plays an essential role in differential-integral calculus. There are various phenomena related to exponential: power law, scale invariance, exponential growth, exponential decay, etc. Here we start from the normal exponential on real number to see its role as the eigenvector of differential-integral calculus and derive its definition in the form of limit. Then using that limit-form definition, we show a deeper meaning of exponential as a mapping from tangent space to the background manifold in general.

The eigenvector of differential-integral calculus

For exponential function \( f(x) = a^x \) with identity \( f(x+y) = a^{x+y} = a^x a^y \), we have its derivative
\[ f'(x) = \frac{\d(a^x)}{\d x} = \lim_{ε\to 0}{\frac{a^{x+ε}-a^{x}}{ε}} = \lim_{ε\to 0}{\frac{a^{x}a^{ε}-a^{x}}{ε}} \] \[ = a^{x}\cdot \left(\lim_{ε\to 0}{\frac {a^{ε}-1}{ε}}\right) \] \[ = f(x)\cdot f'(0) \] where \( f'(0) \) is a constant only depending on the base a. That means the derivative of exponential function is itself scaled by a constant, i.e. exponential is the eigenvector of differential-integral calculus. The scaling constant is the eigenvalue of that eigenvector and is called "rate constant" \( λ = f'(x)/f(x) = f'(0) \), which is the "growth constant" when positive, and the "decay constant" when negative. The natural exponential \( \exp(x) = \e^x \) is defined to have unit rate constant \( \exp'(0) = 1 \), so that it's the unit eigenvector of differential-integral calculus.
\[ \lim_{ε\to 0}{\frac {\e^{ε}-1}{ε}} = 1 \Leftrightarrow \lim_{ε\to 0}{\left(\frac {\e^{ε}-1}{ε} - 1\right)} = 0 \Leftrightarrow \lim_{n\to\infty}{n\cdot( \e^{1/n} - (1+1/n))} = 0 \] ... \[ \Leftrightarrow \lim_{n\to\infty}{\frac{n} {\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \left(\e^{\frac{n-1-k}{n}} (1+\frac{1}{n})^k \right)} \cdot\left(\e - (1+\frac{1}{n})^n \right)} = 0 \]
\[ \Leftarrow \lim_{n\to\infty}{\left(\e - (1+\frac{1}{n})^n \right)} = 0 \]    , because    \[ n < \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \left(\e^{\frac{n-1-k}{n}} (1+\frac{1}{n})^k \right) \]
\[ \Leftarrow \e = \lim_{n\to\infty}{\left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right)^n} \]

With this definition of the constant e (Euler's number), we have
\[ f'(x) = \frac{\d(a^x)}{\d x} = \]  ...  \[ \frac{\d(\e^{x\cdot \ln(a)})}{\d x} = \ln(a) \cdot \frac{\d(\e^{x\cdot \ln(a)})}{\d(x\cdot \ln(a))} = \ln(a) \cdot \e^{x\cdot \ln(a)} \] \[ = \ln(a) \cdot a^x = f(x) \cdot \ln(a) \]
\[ \Leftrightarrow f'(0) = \ln(a) = \lim_{ε\to 0}{\frac {a^{ε}-1}{ε}} \] \[ \Leftrightarrow \lim_{n\to\infty}{n\cdot( a^{1/n} - (1+\ln(a)/n))} = 0 \] ... (similar to the above) \[ \Leftarrow a = \lim_{n\to\infty}{\left(1+\frac{\ln(a)}{n}\right)^n} = \e^α = \lim_{n\to\infty}{\left(1+\frac{α}{n}\right)^n} \]

Exponential map

The expression of \( \e^α \) via limit
\[ \e^α = \exp(α) = \lim_{n\to\infty}{\left(1+\frac{α}{n}\right)^n} \] (Def.1)
shows a deeper meaning of exponential: For any ring \(A \ni α\) endowed with addition, multiplication and scalar multiplication, we can define a function \( \exp(α) \) satisfying exponential identities using Def.1 (see derivation steps below). Moreover, we should consider \(α\) as a parameters of some operator acting on some background object \(T_α(o) = α*o = o' \). For the simplest case of normal numbers, the "background object" is just the unit number "1" and the operator is the normal multiplication \(α\cdot 1 = α\). In exponential growth/decay, the "background object" is the initial value \(x_0\) so that the value at time \(t\) is \(x_t = x_0 e^{λt}\). And in physics, \(α\) is usually a matrix, a complex number, a quaternion, etc. In this sense, we can divide the operator \(T_α\) into \(n\) differentials \(T_{\d α} = (α/n)*\) and then "integrate" them up.

To be general, we consider a background object on a manifold \(o\in M\) and the operator \(T_α\) as a tangent operator of that manifold, i.e. \( \forall o\in M, o + T_{\dα}(o)\in M \). Applying the differential \(T_{\d α}\) to the whole manifold, we have a vector field on \(M\). Then the displacement operator \(X_α(o) = o'\in M\) generated by that vector field, tracing a geodesic \( [o,o'] \subset M\), is expressed as the exponential \(\e^α\). That's because the infinitesimal displacement is \( X_{\dα}(o) = o + T_{\dα}(o) = (1+\dα)*o \), and the whole displacement is the composition of infinitely many infinitesimal displacements \( X_α = X_{\dα}\circ X_{\dα}\circ X_{\dα}\cdots = (1+\dα)\cdot(1+\dα)\cdot(1+\dα)\cdots = e^α \).

In this sense, the exponential is the fundamental building block of differential-integral calculus which is called "exponential map" (in Lie theory as well as in Riemannian geometry). Note that exponential map in dynamical systems is just a mapping from complex plane to itself, which is somehow relevant but different from the exponential map here. In physics, the manifold \(M\) is considered as (phase) space and \(α\) is parameterized by time \(α = α_0\cdot t\), as shown in this video clip, and the operators can act on the whole space just like this visualization of Mandelbrot set.

Identities

Here we derive the identities of exponential directly from the limit definition Def.1 and the expression of composition of operators above.

  • \( e^{0} = 1 \): When \(α=0\), there is no displacement, thus \(e^{0} = X_0\) is an identity operator \(X_0(o) = o\).
  • \( e^{α+β} = e^α\cdot e^β \): The displacement of a sum \(α+β\) is the composition of component displacements \( e^{α+β} = X_{α+β} = X_{α}\circ X_{β} = e^{α}\cdot e^{β} \), just like the parallelogram rule of vector addition (but may not be commutative).
  • \( e^{αt} = (e^α)^t \): The displacement of a parameter \(α\) scaled by a number \(t\) is \(X_α\) raised to the power of \(t\) in the sense of "\(t\) times" (more/less) infinitesimal displacements. \[ e^{αt} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left(1+\frac{αt}{n}\right)^n = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left(1+\frac{α}{n/t}\right)^{(n/t)t} = \left(\lim_{n'\to\infty}\left(1+\frac{α}{n'}\right)^{n'}\right)^t = (e^{α})^t \]
  • \( \d(e^{α}) = e^α\cdot \dα \): Using the differential identity of composition \(\d(X_α\cdot X_β) = X_α\cdot\d X_β + X_β\cdot\d X_α\), we have \[ \d(e^{α}) = \lim_{n\to\infty}\d\left(1+\frac{α}{n}\right)^n = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\left(1+\frac{α}{n}\right)^{n-1}\cdot n\d\frac{α}{n}\right) = \left(\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(1+\frac{α}{n}\right)^{n\frac{n-1}{n}}\right)\cdot\d α = e^α\cdot \dα \] When \(α\) is parameterized by time \(α = α_0\cdot t\) and the tangent \(T_{α_0} = α_0\) is a constant, we have the derivative of displacement \(X_α\) as the composition of the displacement itself with its tangent \(\frac{\d(X_α)}{\d t} = X_α\cdot T_{α_0}\). This property is usually used to solve differential equations, as shown in this video clip.

Taylor series

We can also expand the binomial in Def.1 to retrieve its Taylor series
\[ \exp(α) = \lim_{n\to\infty}{\left(1+\frac{α}{n}\right)^n} = \lim_{n\to\infty} {\sum _{k=0}^{n}{\binom{n}{k} \left(\frac{α}{n}\right)^k}} \]  ...  \[ = \lim_{n\to\infty} {\sum _{k=0}^{n} {\frac{\prod_{j=0}^{k-1}{(n-j)}}{k!} \frac{α^k}{n^k}} } = \lim_{n\to\infty} {\sum _{k=0}^{n} {\left( \frac{α^k}{k!} \cdot \prod_{j=0}^{k-1}{\frac{n-j}{n}} \right)} } \] \[ = \lim_{n\to\infty} {\sum _{k=0}^{n}{\frac{α^{k}}{k!}}} = \sum _{k=0}^{\infty }{\frac{α^{k}}{k!}}=1+α+{\frac {α^{2}}{2}}+{\frac {α^{3}}{6}}+{\frac {α^{4}}{24}}+\cdots \]
